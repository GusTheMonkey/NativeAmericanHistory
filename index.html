Mississippian Culture 700 – 1200

The Mississippian peoples were advanced, hierarchical farming civilizations that had two main classes: elites and non-elites. The elites were believed to be descended from the gods and were thought to have supernatural powers. Their role was to keep order and control the weather to make sure harvests were successful, which was very important for the non-elites who grew corn, the main crop these societies depended on. Cahokia was the largest and most important Mississippian society, reaching its peak around 1100–1300. These civilizations are thought to have declined because of famines and unrest caused by climate change.

Hidatsa Tribe and the Origins of the Crow pre-contact – 1600s

The Hidatsa lived along the upper Missouri River in lodges. They were agricultural and traded with many surrounding nations. Over time, a group within the Hidatsa migrated westward into the open Plains. This migration eventually led to the formation of the Crow people. Though they shared language and ancestry with the Hidatsa, the Crow were nomadic hunters.

No Vitals early 1600s

No Vitals was the chief who led the Hidatsa westward to become the Crow nation. According to tradition, he received a divine vision instructing him to seek a new homeland for his people.

Crow People 1600s – 1800s

The Crow emerged from the western Hidatsa migration and became one of the dominant nations of the Northern Plains. They lived in tipis and moved seasonally with the bison herds. Crow society valued honor and spiritual power, which men had to earn. The introduction of horses revolutionized life for the Crow people, and made raiding and warfare more common.

Blackfoot People pre-contact – 1800s

The Blackfoot were a Confederacy of tribes who lived on the Northern Plains. They were powerful nomadic hunters who relied heavily on bison (similar to the Crow people). Before horses arrived, they hunted on foot with their entire tribe (men and women) in order to get the Bison to charge in one direction so they could isolate them; afterward, mounted hunting transformed their mobility and warfare.

Shawnee People pre-contact – 1800s

The Shawnee were an Algonquian-speaking people originally from the Ohio Valley region. They lived in semi-permanent villages and moved seasonally for hunting and farming. The Shawnee became known to the French as the most powerful and fearsome tribe. Interestingly, the Shawnee spanned a great distance across America but remained unified under the same tribal identity.

Dakota People pre-contact – 1800s

The Dakota are one of the three main divisions of the Sioux Nation, along with the Lakota. The Dakota were semi-sedentary farmers and hunters who relied on corn, wild rice, and game. Their villages and kinship networks formed the foundation of Sioux culture before the westward migrations onto the Plains.

Lakota People 1600s – 1800s

The Lakota were originally part of the larger Dakota nation before migrating west onto the Great Plains. By the 1600s and 1700s, their lifestyle shifted toward buffalo hunting and warfare (similar to the Blackfoot and Crow). The Lakota established powerful bands that controlled vast territories in what is now the Dakotas, Wyoming, and Montana.

Wichita People pre-contact – 1800s

The Wichita were an agricultural, Caddoan-speaking people who lived in grass houses across the southern plains. They had a matrilineal and matrilocal society, with women cultivating crops and men hunting. Their political structure was decentralized, and they were known for their distinctive body art, especially among women. It is believed that the Wichita originally lived farther north but gradually migrated to the southern plains.

Pawnee People Pre-contact – 1800s

The Pawnee were Caddoan-speaking people closely related to the Wichita. The French referred to them as the Panis Blanc (“White Pawnee”) because they practiced less body art than the Wichita.

Caddo People Pre-contact – 1800s

The Caddo were another Caddoan-speaking people related to the Wichita and Pawnee.

Osage People Pre-contact – 1800s

The Osage were Siouan-speaking people who lived in present-day Kansas during the 1600s before relocating to Oklahoma. They were patrilineal but matrilocal and became powerful hunters and traders. The Osage were long-time enemies of the Wichita.

Apache People Pre-contact – 1800s

The Apache lived in small, mobile bands that hunted bison, traded, and raided. The Apache frequently clashed with the Wichita and their neighbors.

Dekanawidah – The Great Peacemaker unknown / ~1400s

Dekanawidah, called the Great Peacemaker, was a spiritual leader who wanted to stop the constant fighting between the Haudenosaunee nations. He convinced the warring nations to come together under the Law of Great Peace, creating the Haudenosaunee or Iroquois Confederacy.

Haudenosaunee Confederacy 1450? – 1650

Somewhere between 1450 and 1650 (or perhaps much earlier), five warring nations established a "Great Law of Peace." This alliance formed what is known as the Iroquois Confederacy, or the Haudenosaunee, meaning people of the longhouse.

  • Mohawk: Protect eastern border
  • Oneida: Eastern mediator
  • Onondaga: Central decision making place
  • Cayuga: Western mediator
  • Seneca: Protect western border

Mourning War Complex pre-colonial – late 1600s

The cycle of cruelty known as the Mourning War Complex was a response to the loss of a community member in a tribe, often due to warfare with rival nations or disease. To restore balance, one tribe would kidnap members of another to replace the lost individual. These captives were often adopted into the tribe, but other times they were ritualistically tortured to death. After the Law of Great Peace, the Mourning War Complex was not committed against other Haudenosaunee nations, though it was still practiced against outsiders.

Fernando de Soto’s Conquest 1539 – 1542

Fernando de Soto was a Spanish conquistador who traveled through the southeastern region of what is now the United States in search of gold. With around 600 men, he devastated many Native populations—raiding, pillaging, spreading disease, and exploiting captives. His expedition left the Southeast in ruins and ended with his death in 1542. His men secretly sank his body in the Mississippi River to prevent local nations from finding it.

Illinois Confederation pre-contact – 1600s

The Cahokia, Kaskaskia, Peoria, Tamaroa, and Michigamea peoples formed what became known as the Illinois Confederation (Illiniwek). These Algonquian-speaking nations shared language, trade, and political unity across the Mississippi River Valley. Their villages and farmlands stretched through modern-day Illinois and parts of Missouri and Iowa.

King Philip (Metacom) 1638 – 1676

Metacom, known to the English as King Philip, was the sachem of the Wampanoag people in what is now New England. At first he attempted to maintain peace with the English colonists, but land loss, cultural pressure, and disputes over authority pushed him into open conflict. His leadership became central to the resistance of Indigenous nations against the English.

The Beaver Wars 1640s – 1701

A series of conflicts driven by the fur trade with European settlers. The Haudenosaunee fought primarily against French-allied tribes (Huron, Algonquin, and others) to control trade routes and access to furs in the northeast. The wars resulted in significant territorial expansion for the Haudenosaunee, but also caused devastating losses and displacement for many Indigenous communities.

King Philip’s War 1675 – 1676

A devastating conflict between the Wampanoag, their Indigenous allies, and the English colonists. It began after years of growing tension over land and colonial control. The war destroyed towns, killed thousands, and left both sides scarred. Metacom was killed in 1676, his death marking the collapse of organized Native resistance in the region. After the war, many surviving Wampanoag were enslaved.

Illinois Visit French Traders 1666

In 1666, about eighty Illinois people traveled up the Mississippi River to a French trading post to meet a Jesuit priest. They explained that their people had long been enemies of the Haudenosaunee, who raided westward seeking captives and control over fur trade routes.

Marquette and the Illinois 1673 – 1675

Jesuit missionary Jacques Marquette met the Illinois people during an expedition to explore the Mississippi River in 1673. He was welcomed by the Kaskaskia near present-day Illinois. Marquette returned in 1674–1675 to offer religious instruction. During that time, the Illinois village’s population had quintupled in size.

The Great Peace of Montreal 1701

The Haudenosaunee, along with 39 other Indigenous nations and the French colonial government, signed the Great Peace of Montreal. This treaty ended decades of conflict in the region and established a framework for peaceful coexistence and trade between the signatory nations.

Pierre Le Moyne d’Iberville 1690s – early 1700s

Pierre Le Moyne d’Iberville, a French explorer and naval officer, helped extend France’s presence through the Mississippi Valley. His expeditions established forts and settlements that connected the Great Lakes region with Louisiana, securing French influence and trade.

Sixth Haudenosaunee Nation 1722

After migrating from North Carolina due to conflict with settlers, the Tuscarora nation joined the Haudenosaunee, becoming the sixth nation of the Iroquois Confederacy.
